# Interface Design

Design-principle guidance distilled from Apple's [Human Interface Guidelines — Design principles](https://developer.apple.com/design/human-interface-guidelines/design-principles). The best designs come from a deep understanding of how people think, feel, and interact. There's no single right way to apply these principles — treat them as tools for weighing competing priorities and making key decisions, not rules to satisfy mechanically.

When designing something new, use the principles below to shape it. When reviewing an existing design, walk the list and flag where it falls short.

## Purpose

- **Create value.** Orient constantly toward what makes the product genuinely useful. At every stage, ask what it is *for* and whether the design serves that purpose.
- **Keep focused.** Prioritize the most important features by aligning with how people want to use the product, and make those features truly great. A product with a clear use helps people meet their goals.
- **Find new ways to solve the problem.** Investigate existing solutions rather than re-creating them. Define what sets the product apart, and let the design reflect that.

## Agency

- **Stay out of the way.** People use a product to get things done — get them directly to the task or content at hand. The best designs are unobtrusive, present only when needed.
- **Give people the freedom to explore.** Don't lock people into specific flows or modes. When a guided flow is necessary, make it easy to skip or escape.
- **Help people recover from mistakes.** Build forgiveness in: reversible actions and easy return to a previous state make an interface inviting to explore. Recovering from the unexpected shouldn't cost people time or work.

## Responsibility

- **Be fully transparent.** Make intentions clear from the first interaction. Provide a clear rationale when asking for permission; when gathering data, say what is collected and how it's used.
- **Keep people's information safe.** Collect only what the product needs to function. Anticipate misuse and harm, and put protections in place.

## Familiarity

- **Use concepts people know.** People bring knowledge of the real world and other software to every new experience — draw on both so the interface feels intuitive.
- **Keep visuals and interactions consistent.** Once a behavior or appearance is established for an element, apply it throughout. Consistency speeds learning and builds confidence that new interactions will work as expected.
- **Provide clear feedback.** Show when controls are available, indicate when content changes, and use system patterns for alerts and choices. Consistent feedback keeps people informed and in control.

## Flexibility

- **Design for everyone.** Consider the diversity of people who may encounter the design — their experiences, perspectives, and needs. Treat accessibility as a priority from the start, not a retrofit.
- **Preserve a person's context.** As the design adapts across platforms and configurations, keep content and controls in consistent, predictable positions, and use natural animations to ease transitions.
- **Consider a variety of input methods.** Voice, touch, keyboard, and more — supporting many inputs lets more people use the product the way that works best for them.
- **Approach every platform with intention.** The software should feel polished and at home wherever it runs; give each supported platform the same level of care.

## Simplicity

- **Include just what's necessary.** Simplicity isn't minimalism. Aim for a focused, useful experience that keeps the important things close by and lets the others fall away.
- **Be concise.** The simplest way to say something is often the most universal and helpful. Choose exactly the words needed to convey a concept or label a control.
- **Establish hierarchy.** Make form and function readily apparent: prioritize recognizable controls and a consistent structure so people understand where they are and what comes next.

## Craft

- **Quality sets the tone.** Every element shows people how much you care. Be deliberate with each decision — visuals, animation, wording, audio.
- **Experiment and iterate.** Prototype early, try new approaches, and discard what doesn't work. Set a high bar, refine, and test in real-world settings for durability, reliability, and performance.
- **Maintain your craft.** Shipping isn't the finish line. Keep the interface current with platform capabilities and design patterns; design is an ongoing commitment.

## Delight

- **Identify the emotion you want to inspire.** A fitness app might energize; a meditation app might calm. Know the feeling you want to evoke, and let it shape the design.
- **Create defining moments.** Every interaction — from a button press to an error message — is a chance to add a touch of character that reflects the spirit of the design.
- **Don't mistake delight for decoration.** People are trying to accomplish a task; don't let delight for its own sake get in the way of the core purpose. Balance practical touches against whimsy by experimenting.
- **Consider the whole.** Delight is the sum of the care put into a product: the freedom to act, the safety to explore, the comfort of familiar metaphors, and flexibility across contexts. Design with intent, focus, and care, and the result feels naturally delightful.
